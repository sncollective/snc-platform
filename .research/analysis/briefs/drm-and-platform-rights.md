# Research: DRM, Monopsony, and Creator Rights

> Status: Complete

## Purpose

This research informs S/NC's charter provisions on:
- Member rights over their own creative work
- S/NC's stance on DRM and content restrictions
- Platform design principles (interoperability, portability, no lock-in)
- Mission language around why S/NC exists

## Key Concepts to Research

### DRM (Digital Rights Management)
- DMCA Section 1201 and how it restricts creative expression beyond copyright
- How DRM harms creators (not just consumers) — e.g. platform lock-in, inability to move work
- EFF's core arguments and proposed alternatives

### Monopsony in Media/Tech
- Single-buyer power: how Spotify, Apple, Amazon have pricing power over creators
- How platform concentration drives down creator revenue
- Cory Doctorow's "enshittification" framework applied to creator platforms

### Interoperability as a Counter to Lock-in
- What interoperability means for creator platforms
- How S/NC could build or advocate for open standards

## Findings

### DRM as Platform Lock-In (EFF)

The standard justification for DRM is creator protection. The EFF's counter: DRM systems are controlled by **platforms and distributors, not creators**. Apple, Amazon, Spotify, Google design and operate the DRM. Individual artists have no control — they simply agree to use it as a condition of distribution.

DRM does not prevent copying (determined actors circumvent it routinely). Its actual function is **platform lock-in**: readers who buy DRM-locked ebooks from Amazon cannot move them to a rival service; Spotify listeners cannot export their library. This benefits platforms, not creators.

### DMCA Section 1201

Federal law making it a crime to circumvent a "technological protection measure" (DRM) even if the underlying act would otherwise be legal. Documented harms:
- Security researchers cannot publish DRM vulnerabilities without liability risk
- Accessibility blocked: blind users using text-to-speech on DRM-locked ebooks technically violate 1201
- Archives and libraries cannot legally preserve DRM-locked media when platforms shut down — content disappears
- Competition suppressed: a competitor building a better player cannot make it compatible with DRM-locked content

### Enshittification (Doctorow)

Doctorow's framework for platform decay:
1. **Good to users** — platform enters market offering genuine value (low prices, creator-friendly terms)
2. **Good to businesses at users' expense** — once users are captured, platform extracts from them to favor business customers (advertisers, publishers)
3. **Good to shareholders at everyone's expense** — once businesses are also locked in, platform extracts from them too. Creator royalty rates fall; take rates rise.

The mechanism enabling all three stages: **switching costs** — data lock-in, network effects, and DRM lock-in. Users and creators cannot leave because they cannot take their content, relationships, and audiences with them.

**Key insight:** DRM is not primarily about piracy prevention. It is about manufacturing switching costs.

### Monopsony in Media (Doctorow)

**Monopsony** = single buyer with pricing power over sellers. Applies to:
- **Spotify** (~31% global music streaming): artists cannot opt out without losing critical revenue. Spotify has used market dominance to reduce per-stream rates as its own revenues grew.
- **Amazon** (~50-70% US ebook sales): pricing power over publishers and, through publishers, authors. "Most favored nation" clauses. Kindle DRM reinforces lock-in.
- **Apple App Store / Google Play**: 30% commission is not competitive pricing — it's the result of a captive distribution channel enforced by DRM and platform policy.
- **YouTube/Instagram/TikTok**: Creators build audiences on platforms that own the follower graph. If the platform changes algorithm or monetization terms, the creator cannot take their audience elsewhere. A labor monopsony.

Key reference: *Chokepoint Capitalism* (Rebecca Giblin & Cory Doctorow, 2022, Beacon Press)

### Creator Economics and the Four-Party Framework (Doctorow)

Doctorow's *Information Doesn't Want to Be Free* (2014) identifies four parties in any copyright transaction: **creators, investors, intermediaries, and audiences**. The roles are fluid — some intermediaries are also investors, some creators are their own distributors — but the structural dynamic is consistent: law and technology tend to favor intermediaries at creators' expense.

**Doctorow's First Law:** "Any time someone puts a lock on something that belongs to you and won't give you the key, that lock isn't there for your benefit." This extends beyond technical DRM to contract clauses, platform policies, and any mechanism that restricts a creator's ability to control their own work. Locks benefit the locksmith — i.e. the platform or distributor — not the creator.

**Choke-point-as-tollbooth:** "Any choke point between the creator and the audience will turn into a tollbooth, where someone will charge whatever the market will bear for the privilege of facilitating the buying and selling of creative work." When distribution channels are scarce and controlled by few players, creators have no negotiating power. The internet opened distribution — but platform consolidation (Spotify, Amazon, Apple) is re-closing it.

**The diversity test:** A good copyright and distribution system enables "the largest diversity of creators making the largest diversity of works to please the largest diversity of audiences." A bad one has fewer creators making fewer types of work. S/NC's success should be measured by how many creators and how many kinds of work the platform enables — not by per-unit revenue extraction.

### Cloud Rent and Platform Feudalism (Varoufakis)

Varoufakis's *Technofeudalism* (2023) argues that major platforms have moved beyond capitalist profit extraction into something structurally feudal. **Cloud rent** — rent paid for access to platform fiefdoms — has replaced profit as the primary extraction mechanism. Artists on Spotify, Amazon, YouTube are not market participants negotiating freely; they are "cloud serfs" contributing unpaid labor (data, attention, content) that enhances the platform's capital while the platform extracts rent from their presence.

Key distinction: capitalist firms are "market-free zones within which a non-market process extracts surplus value from employees." The larger the firm and the more cloud capital it employs, the greater the rents it extracts. This deepens the monopsony analysis above — platforms don't just have pricing power over creators; they have *feudal* power, where removal of a link or algorithmic demotion can destroy a creator's livelihood overnight.

Varoufakis argues that cooperative/democratized firms are actually *more* compatible with genuine markets than capitalist firms — because they eliminate rent-extraction and concentrated market power. "Doing away with capitalist firms, through terminating labour and share markets, paves the ground for truly competitive product markets." This inverts the common objection that cooperatives are anti-market.

### Cultural Context (Fisher)

Fisher's *Capitalist Realism* (2009) identifies the deeper ideological substrate that makes extractive intermediaries seem natural and inevitable: the "widespread sense that not only is capitalism the only viable political and economic system, but also that it is now impossible even to *imagine* a coherent alternative to it."

This matters for S/NC because the strongest defense of platform rent-extraction is not economic but cultural — the assumption that there is no alternative. Fisher warns against "gestural anti-capitalism" — platforms that brand as artist-friendly without structural change (e.g., Bandcamp's ownership changes, Product Red-style "ethical consumerism") actually *reinforce* capitalist realism by performing resistance while preserving extractive structures. S/NC's claim must be structural, not gestural: cooperative ownership, democratic governance, and charter-level commitments that redistribute actual power.

### Interoperability as Counter

Doctorow's proposed solution: **adversarial interoperability** — the right to build systems that interconnect with incumbent platforms without those platforms' permission.

Historical precedents: early email (any client, any server), early web (any browser, any site), VCR recording broadcast TV (Betamax, 1984).

Modern frameworks:
- **ActivityPub / Fediverse**: Decentralized social protocols (Mastodon, etc.) — users can move between servers while keeping their social graph
- **RSS**: Open standard for content syndication without platform lock-in
- **IndieWeb principles**: Creator-owned web presence over platform-dependent distribution

The legal erosion of adversarial interoperability (via DMCA 1201, CFAA expansions, Terms of Service enforcement) is a primary mechanism platforms use to entrench monopsony positions.

## Charter/Principles Implications for S/NC

1. **No proprietary DRM on S/NC-published content** — charter commitment as a condition of platform use
2. **Open licensing default** — Creative Commons as default; creators choose appropriate variant (CC BY, CC BY-NC, etc.); can opt to more restrictive terms for defined reasons
3. **Interoperability commitment** — platform built on open protocols (ActivityPub, RSS, open API); no S/NC feature should create artificial switching costs for members or audiences
4. **Data portability guarantee** — members must always be able to export content, metadata, and relationship graphs in open formats; contractual obligation to members
5. **Anti-monopsony provision** — S/NC may not use platform market position to impose below-market rates on contributing creators; revenue sharing formulas set by member vote, not management
6. **DMCA 1201 advocacy stance** — S/NC takes organizational position supporting Section 1201 reform
