# Playout Content: Public Domain Films

Content sourcing list for the s-nc.tv always-on playout channels. Starting with classic/retro films — expanding to multiple themed channels over time.

## Licensing Rules

Only these licenses are permitted for playout:

- **Public domain** — pre-1928, failed copyright renewal, missing copyright notice, or dedicated to PD
- **CC0** — public domain dedication, no restrictions
- **CC-BY** — attribution required (include in channel overlay or credits rotation)
- **CC-BY-SA** — attribution + share-alike (same as above, note share-alike obligation if remixing)

**Not permitted:** CC-BY-NC (non-commercial clause is ambiguous for platform use and we're avoiding it entirely).

## How Public Domain Status Works (US)

### Guaranteed public domain

- **Published before 1928** — copyright expired as of January 1, 2023. No verification needed.

### Requires verification

- **Published 1928-1963, copyright not renewed** — US copyright law required renewal after 28 years. If the rights holder didn't file a renewal with the Copyright Office, the work entered the public domain. This is the most common reason post-1927 films are PD, and the most common source of mistakes.
- **Published 1928-1977, no copyright notice** — works published without a valid copyright notice (© or "Copyright" + year + owner) entered the public domain immediately. Example: Night of the Living Dead (1968) — the distributor removed the notice from the title card.
- **Published 1978-1988, no notice + no registration** — the 1976 Copyright Act (effective 1978) was more forgiving, but works published without notice and not registered within 5 years still entered PD.

### How to verify film copyright renewal

A film registered in year X needed renewal in year X+28. No renewal = public domain.

**Where to search:**

1. **Catalog of Copyright Entries (CCE), Class L (photoplays)** — the authoritative source. Published volumes cover all film registrations and renewals. Look up the renewal year (registration + 28), Class L, search for the title.
   - Digitized volumes on **HathiTrust** and **Internet Archive** — search "catalog of copyright entries motion pictures". Full-text searchable scans.
   - **UPenn Online Books CCE project** (`onlinebooks.library.upenn.edu/cce/`) — partially digitized and searchable online, includes some motion picture classes. Coverage may be incomplete.

2. **US Copyright Office COCATALOG** (`cocatalog.loc.gov`) — searchable records from **1978 onward only**. Useful if a renewal was filed after 1978, but won't cover most films in our list.

3. **Copyright Office paid search** — they search the complete pre-1978 card catalog for ~$200/hour. The nuclear option for high-value titles where CCE scans are inconclusive.

**Other references (not authoritative, but useful for cross-checking):**

- **Duke Law Public Domain Day** (`web.law.duke.edu/cspd/`) — annual lists of works entering PD with legal analysis
- **Wikipedia's list of public domain films** — crowd-sourced, cites sources, good starting point
- **David Pierce's film preservation research** (Library of Congress) — film historian who has done extensive copyright status analysis

### Foreign film complications (GATT/URAA)

The Uruguay Round Agreements Act (1996) restored US copyright for certain foreign works that had entered the US public domain. A foreign film that was PD in the US due to non-renewal or missing notice may have had its copyright restored if:

1. The work was still under copyright in its country of origin on January 1, 1996
2. The work had entered the US public domain for reasons other than expiration

This primarily affects European and Asian films from the 1930s-1960s. Always check GATT restoration status for non-US films. Upheld by the Supreme Court in *Golan v. Holder* (2012) — settled law.

**The exception that matters:** If a foreign film's copyright had *also* expired in its home country by Jan 1, 1996, GATT couldn't restore it. This is why early Soviet films (50-year corporate term) are the richest foreign PD vein — many expired in Russia before the GATT date.

### What GATT kills

These categories are widely circulated on cheap DVDs but are almost certainly NOT public domain:

- **Japanese golden age** — Kurosawa, Ozu, Mizoguchi. Japan has life+70 years. All GATT-restored.
- **Italian neorealism** — Rossellini, De Sica, Fellini. Italy has life+70. All GATT-restored.
- **French New Wave** — Godard, Truffaut. France has life+70. All GATT-restored.
- **Spaghetti westerns** — Leone. Still copyrighted in Italy. GATT-restored.
- **Classic kung fu / martial arts** — Shaw Brothers, Golden Harvest, Bruce Lee. Hong Kong has life+50 (corporate 50 from publication). Everything from the 60s-70s was still copyrighted in HK on the GATT date. Celestial Pictures actively enforces the Shaw Brothers library. Budget DVD compilations are unauthorized, not PD.
- **British Hitchcock (post-1927)** — UK has life+70. The 39 Steps, The Lady Vanishes, etc. were likely GATT-restored.

## Gotchas

**Restored/remastered versions may carry new copyrights.** A 4K scan with new color grading, new score, or new editorial choices can be copyrighted as a derivative work even if the underlying film is PD. Use transfers that don't claim new creative rights — straight telecines, preservation scans, and archive.org community uploads are generally safe. Criterion Collection and similar boutique releases are NOT safe to use.

**Soundtrack rights are separate.** A film's visuals can be PD while its musical score remains copyrighted. This mainly affects:
- Films with licensed popular music (rare in the PD-era catalog)
- Re-scored versions (e.g., modern scores added to silent films)
- Commissioned scores that were separately copyrighted

For silent films, use versions with their original scores or no score — we can add CC-licensed music via the playout system if needed.

**Some archive.org uploads are misidentified.** Cross-reference PD status independently. Don't assume an archive.org upload is PD just because it's there.

**Colorized versions are derivative works.** Always use the original B&W version unless the colorization is also PD/CC-licensed.

### Foreign: Soviet Sound Era (1928-early 1940s)

The unique opportunity. Soviet films were state-produced (corporate authorship). Russia's 1993 copyright law gave 50 years from publication for corporate works. Films published before ~1943-1946 had expired Russian copyright by Jan 1, 1996 — GATT couldn't restore them. Russia later extended to 70 years (2004), but that's after the GATT date and doesn't affect the analysis.

**Caveat:** WWII-era works (1941-1945) may get a 4-year extension under Russian law, pushing edge cases past the GATT date. Films from 1942-1945 need careful verification.

### Not Available: Martial Arts / Kung Fu

Investigated thoroughly — **no classic martial arts films are in the US public domain.** The entire Shaw Brothers, Golden Harvest, and classic Hong Kong catalogs are GATT-restored. Pre-1949 Chinese martial arts films theoretically could qualify, but surviving viewable prints are virtually nonexistent.

If we want martial arts content for a future channel, it requires licensing.
