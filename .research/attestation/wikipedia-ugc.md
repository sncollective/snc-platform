# User-Generated Content — Wikipedia

## Paraphrased Summary

Wikipedia's article on UGC covering definition, platform liability regimes, rights/attribution considerations for derivative content, and governance models.

## Definition

The OECD framework requires three elements for UGC:
1. Publicly accessible content on internet platforms
2. Creative effort that adds value
3. Creation outside professional contexts

Content types include images, videos, audio, text, testimonials, software, and user interactions.

## Platform Liability Models

**United States — Section 230:** Platforms receive broad immunity — "no provider or user of an interactive computer service shall be treated as the publisher or speaker" of user content, even if harmful. Exception if platforms promise removal then fail to comply.

**DMCA Safe Harbor:** Platforms get safe harbor if they promptly remove allegedly infringing materials upon notice, provided they lack actual knowledge of infringement.

**UK/EU:** The Defamation Act (1996) exempts ISPs lacking knowledge or "effective control" over content. EU Electronic Commerce Directive uses horizontal liability standards.

## Derivative Content and Rights

The article distinguishes transformative fair use from simple republication: "Uploading photographs, expressing one's thoughts in a blog post or creating a new music video could be considered UGC," whereas "copying a portion of a television show and posting it to an online video website" typically wouldn't qualify as UGC.

## Moderation Approaches

Human-centered and machine-centered assessment methods described:
- Community-based ranking (wisdom of crowds)
- User-based personalization
- Designer-based diversity maximization for content curation

## Governance Models

- **Wikipedia:** Community collaborative governance
- **Second Life:** Economic community participation through virtual asset creation and trading
